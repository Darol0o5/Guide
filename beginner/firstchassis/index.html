    Choosing Your First Chassis

    Choosing Your First Chassis¶

    1. If you have no previous experience with RC drift or other RC categories, I would not recommend starting with the most expensive chassis available.

    Even if a high-end kit keeps its resale value, you may still damage it simply because you lack experience. There is also a chance that the hobby may not be for you, so going all-in from day one is not always the smartest choice.

    If you end up loving the hobby, you will almost certainly want another chassis sooner or later anyway. Starting with a more beginner-friendly option is not a waste — it gives you the experience to properly build, tune and drive a better kit later.

    2. If you have absolutely no RC experience and do not feel confident with mechanics, your safest choice is usually an RTR or ARTR car with a working factory setup. A used car can also be a good option, but without experience it may be difficult to tell whether the car has a problem, or whether you simply do not yet have the skill to drive it properly.

    3. Many people ask: “What is the best chassis?” There is no single correct answer.

    A perfectly machined, high-end CNC kit with top electronics can still perform badly if it is assembled or tuned incorrectly. At the same time, an experienced driver with a DIY K969 conversion built from spare parts may drive with better angle, consistency and control.

    Chassis can be compared by precision, fitment quality, adjustability, parts support, availability and value. But some kits require more understanding and are better suited to experienced builders.

    Try not to overwhelm yourself with too many advanced tuning options at the beginning. More adjustment is not always better if you do not yet understand what those adjustments do. Beginners often assume that buying upgrades will automatically improve the car. In reality, many upgrades are cosmetic, while others simply unlock additional tuning options. Without understanding how to use those options, an upgrade can sometimes make the car perform worse. In fact, a simpler chassis with fewer tuning options is often easier to learn and can provide a much more enjoyable experience for beginners.

    Build your experience one step at a time. Learn to drive first, then learn to tune, then decide what kind of chassis philosophy fits you best.

    Take a look at the Catalogue (WIP). It is not intended to tell you what to buy. Instead, it is designed to help you compare different chassis, understand their strengths, weaknesses and available adjustments, and make an informed decision based on your own needs and budget.

    It can also be used simply as a historical reference to see how small scale RWD drift chassis evolved over time.
