    How RWD came to small scale drifting¶

    2014 Early experiments – Afada Rioo¶

    When I first asked myself how RWD drifting actually entered the small-scale RC world, the most logical first guess was Kyosho Mini-Z, since the platform has existed for many years. But the truth turned out to be different — the first commercial small-scale RWD chassis wasn’t Kyosho at all, but the Atomic DRZ.

    That alone didn’t satisfy me, so I went digging deeper — year by year, post by post. Eventually I reached the very beginning: 2014, inside the Facebook group Mini R/C Drift Hobby Indonesia.

    There, almost everything related to RWD in small scale was posted or created by one person:

    Afada Rioo — the pioneer.

    While going through the group chronology from back then, you can almost feel how the whole community was waiting for his next update. Afada set himself a challenge — and fully committed to it. Of course, by today’s standards his results look primitive, but he did it, and more importantly — he inspired everyone else.

    Reading his posts felt like a primate trying to understand quantum physics. It was a completely different time — no precise nano-electronics, no ready-made components. He had to invent solutions entirely on his own.

    The next one was created later, but still… what even is this?

    Afada had to learn everything at once — electronics, mechanics, geometry, experimentation. He modified how components worked, rebuilt systems from scratch, and constantly adapted new ideas.

    Today we can confidently say that he is the grandfather of small-scale RWD drifting, and the connection between his early experiments and what exists today is truly fascinating.

    2016 Legowo Setiawan and his first K989 conversion¶

    Legowo Setiawan had been an active 1/10 RC drifter for years, studying chassis construction, steering geometry, suspension behavior, and tuning. In 2015 he decided to apply that knowledge — along with some of Afada Rioo’s discoveries — and began his own RWD project.

    The WLToys K989 is a budget “semi-hobby-grade” chassis, yet in 2016, Setiawan managed to turn it into a functional RWD drift platform.

    A year later, in 2017, he successfully converted the Atomic AMR into an RWD drifter as well — and this time, it didn’t go unnoticed.

    Let’s return to the importance of Afada Rioo. Look at these two cars side by side — do you recognize the connection?

    Setiawan received official support from Atomic. Their manager, Mr. Kim, began developing a new kit based on Legowo’s work.

    Legowo built two prototypes:

    • one was sent to Atomic

    • the other he continued developing independently

    According to him, both DRZ1 and DRZ2 are direct results of their collaboration. However, he shared that at the end of 2018, Atomic unilaterally ended the contract and has not responded to his messages since.

    I also attempted to contact Mr. Kim — but so far without success.

    One thing, however, is undeniable:

    Legowo Setiawan and Mr. Kim are the two people responsible for bringing the first commercial small-scale RWD drift chassis into existence — the Atomic DRZ.

    2018 Birth of the Atomic DRZ¶

    • On January 6, 2018, Atomic officially announced the DRZ on Facebook.

    • Sales began in March.

    For the first time, the world had a dedicated RWD small-scale drift chassis.

    May 2019 XRX Techstorm DPA¶

    XRX entered the scene firing upgrades like a machine gun — custom electronics, and a full line of high-end replacement components machined from 7075-T6 anodized aluminum.

    June 2019 Super Skeeter conversion by BM Racing¶

    BM Racing began a partially collaborative project with Atomic, aiming to improve the DRZ platform.

    On June 12, 2019, they released the Super Skeeter conversion, offering:

    • direct-drive steering

    • extremely easy wheelbase adjustment

    • quick caster adjustment

    • adjustable Ackermann

    It was shiny, beautifully machined, high quality, and performed wonderfully. Anyone interested in small-scale drifting back then wanted one — and how could you not? Just look at it:

    September 2019 - DriftArt¶

    In September 2019, when the main figures of the small scale scene were already clearly established, another extremely important chassis entered the stage — DriftArt, developed by Mr. Chen.

    DriftArt stood out sharply from everything known up to that point. Its ultra-low profile, strongly reminiscent of a Formula 1 car, made it instantly recognizable. The chassis was manufactured using nylon 3D printing, which at the time was new and innovative approach in the small scale RWD drift world.

    The entire design philosophy was focused on minimalism, a low center of gravity, and visual realism. DriftArt allowed MINI-Z bodies to be mounted in a way that fully concealed the chassis beneath the body, resulting in a highly realistic appearance.

    From a technical standpoint, the first generation DriftArt offered an impressive set of solutions:

    • helical gears

    • a wide range of ride height adjustments

    • telescopic CVDs

    • multiple components assembled using a tongue-and-groove system, allowing adjustment and disassembly without the need for tools

    • support for direct installation of a standard MINI-Z body mount

    • an integrated universal body mount, providing full adjustability

    The first generation DriftArt was not a mass-produced product. Approximately 200 units were manufactured and sold exclusively in China, which further contributed to its almost mythical status among collectors and early enthusiasts.

    Despite its limited availability, DriftArt clearly demonstrated the direction in which small scale RWD drifting was evolving — bold design choices, innovative technologies, and an uncompromising pursuit of optimal geometry. This first generation laid the foundation for later DriftArt versions, which would go on to become some of the most influential chassis in the history of small scale RWD drifting.

    What makes the first-generation DriftArt even more remarkable is that, in at least one well-known case, a long-time DriftArt collector — owning nearly every DriftArt component ever produced — openly expressed willingness to purchase a DriftArt 1 for $1,000, yet was still unable to acquire one.

    As of today, it is believed that all surviving DriftArt 1 chassis remain in China, held by private collectors, further emphasizing their extreme rarity and almost inaccessible status.

    November 2019 - TRC¶

    In November 2019, another exceptionally rare and ambitious chassis appeared: TRC V1, developed by Tommy RC.

    Unlike most designs of the period, TRC V1 was never intended as a mass-market product. It was released in an extremely limited production run of only 30 units, split evenly between two colors — 15 pink and 15 purple — instantly placing it among the rarest small scale RWD drift chassis ever produced.

    TRC V1 represents one of the most experimental engineering approaches seen before the COVID era. Designed in China and sold from China, the chassis is reported to have been manufactured in Japan and constructed using high-grade aluminum components, reflecting a no-compromise philosophy focused on precision rather than accessibility.

    Technically, TRC V1 stands out for its highly unconventional geometry. The front suspension features active caster, while the rear incorporates a dynamic rear toe system mechanically linked to steering input, highlighting an advanced and experimental attempt to influence rear-wheel behavior during cornering rather than relying on fixed geometry. This level of mechanical complexity was virtually unheard of in small scale RWD designs at the time.

    Additional details reported by documented owners include aluminum oil-filled shocks, adjustable wheelbase ranging from 90 to 120 mm, and a construction that prioritized adjustability, mechanical feedback, and stability over simplicity.

    Due to its extremely limited production and lack of official documentation, TRC V1 remains largely undocumented and almost unseen outside of China, with very few verified sightings worldwide. Its rarity, combined with its bold engineering choices, has given TRC V1 an almost mythical status within the small scale RWD drift community.

    Rather than influencing the scene through widespread adoption, TRC V1 stands as a peak example of late-2019 experimental design — a chassis that pushed mechanical ideas to their limits just before small scale RWD drifting entered its next phase of rapid global expansion.

    COVID-19 and the boom of home drifting¶

    When the COVID-19 pandemic hit in 2020, nearly all major drift tracks closed overnight. For the 1/10 scene this was a setback — but for the small-scale scene, it became an unexpected accelerator.

    People were stuck at home with:

    • limited space

    • limited budget

    • lots of free time

    Many drifters who previously used only 1/10 scale began searching for something they could run in the living room, bedroom, or even on a desk.

    Small-scale RWD drifting fit perfectly.

    Social media filled with homemade tracks built from:

    • PVC sheets

    • carpet pieces

    • vinyl flooring

    • whiteboards and tables

    Creativity exploded.

    Online communities grew rapidly. Build logs, tuning experiments, 3D-printed chassis, DIY electronics — the pace was incredible.

    For the first time, drifting shifted from “the track” to “the home laboratory.”

    Since small-scale chassis require little space and parts are more affordable, the barrier to entry nearly disappeared. Many people who never considered RC drifting before joined the hobby — and stayed.

    The pandemic didn’t just give the hobby a push — it globalized and accelerated the growth of small-scale drifting, transforming it into a full ecosystem:

    • independent designers

    • home builders

    • tuning specialists

    • 3D-printed platforms

    • dedicated electronics

    • hundreds of small communities worldwide

    This was the moment when the niche became a movement.

    Conclusion¶

    Small-scale RWD drifting didn’t appear out of nowhere. It emerged thanks to a handful of passionate experimenters who worked in their homes, shared discoveries online, and expanded the limits of what was possible.

    From Afada Rioo’s early experiments in 2014, to Legowo Setiawan’s conversions, to the release of the Atomic DRZ — each step built on the previous one. Independent designers, new conversions, XRX, and BM Racing further pushed the scene forward.

    By the time the pandemic arrived, the foundation was already there — and the world, suddenly stuck at home, gave the hobby the perfect conditions to blossom.

    This story isn’t just about chassis and electronics. It’s about creativity, curiosity, and a community that grew from a few experiments into a global movement.
