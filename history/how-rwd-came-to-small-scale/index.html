    How RWD came to small scale drifting¶

    2014 Early experiments – Afada Rioo¶

    Imagine searching by keyword RWD at all mini rc groups by year until there's no results, and the earliest i found it mentioned was 2014. But imagine that at the group Mini R/C Drift Hobby Indonesia, everything according to RWD was either made or posted by Afada Rioo. He was a pioneer at the time! Going trough the posts, i felt the anticipation of each next post of Afada Rioo by the community. He challenged himself and accepted his own challenge! Not by any means of today's standard, but still...he made it, and not only by making his project work, but more importantly, by inspiring everyone else. I recall going through his posts, it seemed like he was explaining rocket science. It was different than it's now. He couldn't just buy precise nano electronics, because there weren't any.     2016 Legowo Setiawan and the first K989 conversion¶

    Legowo Setiawan was into 1/10 rc drift and he studied the constructions and how stuff worked there. In 2015 he decided to to use what he's learnd and some of Afada Rioo's discoveries and started his work on rwd project. During 2016, he managed to convert Wltoys K989 to RWD drift car. Inspired to go even further, In 2017 he successfully converted Atomic AMR to RWD drifter and that didn't go unnoticed.

    I told you Afada Rioo's work was important. Now look at this two cars together.

    Legowo received an endorsement from Atomic. The manager, mr.Kim, started developing a kit based on his build. Legowo made two prototype kits: one of them, he sent to Atomic, and the other, he continued developing himself. According to mr.Setiawan, DRZ1 and DRZ2 are result from his research with Atomic, but at the end of 2018, Atomic unilaterally ended the contract with him and still haven't responded to his last message. Hearing both sides would be valuable for historical accuracy, but mr.Kim haven't replyed to me either. Yet one thing is certain. It's mr.Legowo Setiawan and mr.Kim that are responsible for the existence of the first dedicated small scale, rear wheel drive, rc drift chassis.

    COVID-19 and the boom of home drifting¶

    When the COVID-19 pandemic hit in early 2020, almost every RC drift track in the world closed overnight. For the 1/10 scale scene, this was a major setback — but for small scale drifting, it unexpectedly became a catalyst.

    People were stuck at home with limited space, limited budget, and a lot of time.
    Many hobbyists who previously drove only 1/10 cars suddenly looked for something they could use indoors, on small layouts, even on their living room floor.

    Small scale RWD drift fit perfectly into that gap.

    Social media groups started filling with homemade tracks made from: - EVA foam mats
    - cardboard barriers
    - wooden tiles
    - even whiteboards or polished tables

    The creativity exploded.

    Online communities grew rapidly.
    Build logs, setup experiments, DIY chassis mods and custom electronics were being shared at a pace never seen before.
    For the first time, drifting went from being “track-based” to being a “home lab” hobby.

    And because small scale cars consume very little space and parts are more affordable, newcomers felt no barrier to entry.
    People who never considered RC drifting before suddenly got into it — and stayed.

    COVID-19 didn’t just push the hobby forward.
    It worldwide accelerated small scale RWD drifting into a full ecosystem of: - independent designers
    - home builders
    - tuning experiments
    - 3D printed chassis
    - specialized electronics
    - small local communities

    It was the moment the niche became a movement.

    Summary¶

    Small scale RWD drifting did not appear out of nowhere.
    It was shaped by a handful of passionate individuals experimenting in their homes, sharing their discoveries online, and pushing the limits of what was possible in miniature scale.

    From Afada Rioo’s pioneering attempts in 2014, to Legowo Setiawan’s creative conversions, to Atomic’s DRZ bringing a dedicated RWD chassis to the world — every step built on the previous one.
    Independent designers, new conversion kits, and later XRX and BMHobby’s contributions expanded the scene even further.

    By the time the pandemic hit, the foundation was already there — and the global shift to indoor hobbies finally allowed small scale RWD drifting to bloom.

    This history is not just about chassis and electronics.
    It’s about creativity, curiosity, and a community that grew from a few experiments into a worldwide movement.
